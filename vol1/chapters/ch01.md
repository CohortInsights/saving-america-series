# Chapter 1


## Plot Summary


Kep Westegard, a fiercely competitive thirteen-year-old swimmer, has never willingly missed a day of swim training. This summer, however, he sacrifices time in the pool to attend Forty Liberty, a Revolutionary War reenactment camp in Wisconsin. Kep hopes to win the camp’s $2,000 prize in order to replace money stolen from him during a burglary several months earlier.


Kep attends the camp with his younger brother Max, who earned a scholarship after winning a national history award. The Westgards could not otherwise have afforded to attend. Max’s academic enthusiasm contrasts with Kep’s more athletic focus. At home Max spends much of his time reading glamorous history books and conducting science experiments, while Kep devotes himself to competitive swimming.


The boys join other campers in historically accurate military drills such as marching and drumming. The other two members of the team are TJ, a Black teen with a flair for drama, and Tela, a determined girl who often challenges the roles assigned to her.


During a mock battle exercise, the reenactment becomes chaotic. Kep struggles to determine whether the events are part of the simulation or something more dangerous. When soldiers dressed as British redcoats burst from a captured fort, Kep hears gunshots and sees participants falling and calling for help.


In the chaos Kep sees a soldier charging toward Max with a bayonet. Acting instinctively, Kep tackles the attacker to protect his brother, cutting his own hand in the process. The exercise abruptly ends when the staff reveal that the weapons were firing special foam bullets as part of the reenactment.


Kep is reprimanded for dropping the flag during the struggle. As his wound is treated by the camp medic Mule, readers learn about the roles of various adults in the camp. The chapter ends when TJ bursts into the tent to announce that Tela has challenged the boys to a showdown, declaring she is tired of being assigned “girl duties.”

## Reader Revelation Device

**New Clues Introduced**

* Rumors circulate about a “time-traveling army.”
* Max’s father studies theoretical time travel.
* The reenactment exercises are conducted with unusually high realism and discipline.
* Only four children appear to be participating in the camp despite the presence of many adult reenactors. Clue from Mule's remark: “They hired us reenactors to train you kids like we were training you for the real thing.” 
* Although Kep continues to interpret the camp primarily as a competition, these hints suggest that the program may have a purpose beyond simple historical reenactment.

## Historical Themes and Details

**Loss of Modern Conveniences.** The camp emphasizes the absence of modern comforts that people take for granted today. Items such as toothpaste, deodorant, sunscreen, electric lighting, flush toilets, and toilet paper did not exist for soldiers in the eighteenth century. Historical substitutes such as corncobs (instead of toilet paper!) illustrate the rough living conditions of the period.

**Infantry Roles.** Revolutionary War units relied on specialized duties such as drummers, fifers, and flag bearers. These roles were essential for communication and coordination during battle, allowing commanders to signal movements across the battlefield.

**Youth in the Revolutionary War.** The story highlights the fact that some soldiers in the war were very young. Max notes that the youngest soldier killed in the Revolutionary War was a thirteen-year-old drummer, illustrating the dangerous responsibilities carried by youth in that era.

**Eighteenth-Century Medicine.** Mule’s treatment of Kep’s wound introduces period medical practices. The use of leeches reflects common medical techniques of the time, and cauterizing a wound with a heated spoon is mentioned as another historically used method.
