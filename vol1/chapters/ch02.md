# Chapter 2

### Plot Summary

Life at the Forty Liberty Revolutionary War reenactment camp begins to reveal more about the campers and their motivations. Kep and Max remain focused on the eleven-day competition and the $2,000 prize that Kep hopes will replace the money stolen from him during a burglary several months earlier.

TJ speaks about his family’s deep connection to Black American history. While he participates enthusiastically in the reenactment, he wonders whether an authentic eighteenth-century army would have allowed him to serve as a soldier. Unlike Kep, TJ is less motivated by the prize money and more interested in the possibility that the competition might lead to a role in a television reality show. He explains that in his home “every month is Black History Month.”

Readers also learn more about Tela, the outspoken daughter of a wealthy senator. Tela is passionate about animal rights and promotes veganism, sometimes irritating other campers with her activism.

Kep visits the camp office and calls his mother, Barbara, hoping for news about the burglary at their home. He learns that the police have made no progress in solving the crime. Barbara also reveals that she lost a treasured ring during the robbery. The theft appears unusual because, along with money and jewelry, the burglar also stole items such as family toothbrushes and several of Kep’s father’s drawings about time travel.

Back at camp, tensions rise when Commander Bombast criticizes Tela for wearing modern clothing rather than historically accurate garments suitable for a laundress. Tela argues that women did participate in the Revolutionary War and challenges the camp’s best marksman, Murphy, to a shooting contest.

Ramsey prepares the targets, including an ace of spades nailed to a tree and a candle whose flame must be shot out. Both Murphy and Tela successfully shoot the ace of spades during the first round, but Tela ultimately wins the contest, earning the admiration of the crowd.

During the competition Kep learns that Tela’s family home had also been robbed and that toothbrushes were stolen there as well. The similarity to his own family’s burglary makes Kep suspect the crimes may somehow be connected.

Later Kep reflects on Ramsey’s sleep-talk about a “time traveling army.” Combined with the stolen time-travel drawings and the strange burglaries, the idea begins to feel less like nonsense and more like part of a deeper mystery.

### Character Perspectives

**Kep**  
**What We Learn.** We learn more about his motivation for the prize money coming from losing $1938
in a strange home burgarly. He is disappointed that the money has not been recovered. He was going
to attend an elite swim camp using the funds.
**Understanding of Events.** Begins to suspect that the burglary at his home may be connected to something larger.

**TJ**  
**What We Learn.** Interested in attention and the possibility of appearing in a television reality show.  
**Understanding of Events.** Participates enthusiastically in the reenactment but also reflects on how race would have affected his role in a real eighteenth-century army.

**Max**  
**What We Learn.** Curious and intellectually engaged with history and science.  
**Understanding of Events.** Observes events quietly and continues to experience the camp primarily as a historical learning opportunity.

**Tela**  
**What We Learn.** Driven by strong moral convictions about animal rights and equality. 
**Understanding of Events.** Challenges both gender expectations and camp authority while demonstrating impressive skill as a marksman.

**Minor Characters**

* **Ramsey** — Shows interest in the crowd and in the competition.
* **Barbara** — Kep's mother, who we learn is an orphan raised by nuns.
* **Murphy** — Marksman who is gracious to Tela in defeat.
---
### Reader Revelation Device

**New Clues Introduced**

* The burglar stole unusual personal items such as toothbrushes and family pictures.
* Kep’s father’s drawings about time travel were taken during the robbery.
* Tela’s family experienced a similar burglary involving toothbrushes.
* Ramsey reportedly sleep-talks about a “time traveling army.”

These clues suggest that the burglaries may be connected to something more deliberate than simple theft. The stolen personal items and time-travel research hint that the crimes may be linked to a larger plan connected to the camp.

### Emerging Questions

* Why were toothbrushes and other personal items stolen during multiple burglaries?
* Why were Kep’s father’s time-travel drawings taken?
* Are the burglaries connected to the camp or to Ramsey’s references to a “time traveling army”?
* What larger purpose might the camp serve beyond the reenactment competition?
