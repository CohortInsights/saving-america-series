# Chapter 3

### Plot Summary

The chapter opens with another incident involving Tela’s activism when chickens are released from their coop. The cook confronts Tela and accuses her of cutting the wires. Tela defends her actions by arguing that living creatures should not be treated as property. Kep later suggests she might consider moderating her activism, noting that a vegan girl on his swim team lives her beliefs without constantly confronting others. Tela rejects this idea, insisting that quiet personal choices do not change society.

Later TJ speaks with a reenactor named Hamer about race and the Revolutionary War. Hamer explains that thousands of Black soldiers served in Washington’s army, sometimes fighting for freedom while still enslaved themselves. TJ responds by describing how his grandfather served as an officer in World War II but was not allowed into the officers’ club because he was Black. Kep reflects that judging people by the color of their skin seems like the ultimate form of cheating.

Earlier in the day Kep had wandered away from the camp in search of a place to swim. While exploring the woods he noticed modern surveillance cameras and electrical warning signs that seemed out of place in a supposedly eighteenth-century environment.

While hiding near the lake afterward, Kep overhears a conversation between Querishi and Sergeant Turner. Their discussion reveals that a detective investigating the burglary at Kep’s house has arrived at the camp and suspects a connection between the crime and the camp itself. Querishi worries that someone may have been planted inside the camp and refers to a mysterious figure named Fox.

When a messenger reports that part of Fox’s code has been deciphered, the adults realize that Fox has obtained maps of Boston from 1775. Querishi fears that Fox intends to interfere with Paul Revere’s midnight ride and thereby prevent the American Revolution from beginning. She also expresses concern about sending “four children against a man like Fox.”

Kep misunderstands much of what he hears and assumes the adults must be discussing some kind of elaborate competition scenario.

That evening the boys are surprised by a visit from TJ’s mother, who explains that she was nearby and wanted to check on her son. During the visit she recognizes Kep’s family name and recalls having worked with his father years earlier in a laboratory before becoming a patent lawyer.

Later that night someone enters the boys’ cabin and triggers the alarm. TJ discovers that his handkerchief is missing. Max speculates that the earlier thefts of toothbrushes and handkerchiefs might have been attempts to collect DNA samples.

The chapter ends dramatically when Sergeant Turner awakens the boys and urges them to evacuate as explosions light up the night sky and the fort appears to be under attack.

### Character Perspectives

**Kep**  
**What We Learn.** We learn that a psychologist told his parents that she believes Kep is obsessed with Olympic competition because of feelings of inferiority about his poor academic performance versus his brother.  
**Understanding of Events.** Notices strange inconsistencies such as modern surveillance equipment but assumes they relate to the camp’s elaborate competition.

**TJ**  
**What We Learn.** More about the background of his family and their service in the face of racial discrimination.  
**Understanding of Events.** Reflects on historical issues of race but otherwise treats events at the camp as part of the reenactment environment.

**Max**  
**What We Learn.** His fears of the burglary manifest in the installation of a new "alarm system" in the camp.  
**Understanding of Events.** Begins forming more systematic explanations for the strange burglaries, suggesting stolen items might be used for DNA collection.

**Tela**  
**What We Learn** Tela's zealotry is brought out again in this chapter. The reader suspects that
her strong ethical beliefs are to play a role in the future plot but its present emphasis is not clear.  
**Understanding of Events.** Continues challenging authority and defending her belief that activism requires confrontation.

#### Minor characters
* Fox: A mysterious figure who may be attempting to interfere with history. Being set up as an antagonist to the teens
* Turner and Querishi: Both seem to know information about Fox that is unclear to Kep, the reader, and even other
members of the re-enactment group.

### Reader Revelation Device

**New Clues Introduced**

* Modern surveillance cameras and high-voltage warning signs exist near the camp.
* Camp staff are intercepting and decoding messages from a mysterious figure named Fox.
* Fox may be attempting to interfere with Paul Revere’s midnight ride.
* The adults discuss sending “four children” to confront Fox.
* Additional personal items such as TJ’s handkerchief are stolen.

These clues strongly suggest that the camp is involved in something far more serious than a historical reenactment. The discussion about protecting Paul Revere’s ride implies that the adults believe real historical events may be at risk, though Kep continues to interpret everything as part of the camp’s elaborate competition.

---
### Historical Themes & Details

**Race and American History.** 
Thousands of slaves fought in the Revolutionary War and were forced back into servitude after its conclusion. Similarly, TJ's grandfather served as an officer in the WWII army and was not allowed into the officers' club because he was Black. We suspect that TJ's being black is going to be an important factor in the future plot of 18th century Boston.

### Emerging Questions

* Who is Fox and how is he attempting to interfere with history?
* Why would the camp rely on children rather than adults to oppose him?
* Why are personal items such as toothbrushes and handkerchiefs being collected?
* What is the significance of the connection between Kep’s father and TJ’s mother?
* Why does the camp maintain modern surveillance and security while presenting itself as a reenactment environment?
