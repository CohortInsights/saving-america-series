# Chapter 6

## Plot Summary

The team rushes to make preparations to sneak TJ aboard the time-travel machine. Kep’s first attempt to find a hiding place inside the transport module fails when a worker quickly notices him and tells him the area is not authorized. On the way out, however, Kep feels cool air coming from a large wall vent. The two-by-three-foot passage behind the vent appears to be an ideal hiding place for someone TJ’s size.

Forced to leave the area, Kep returns to the others and explains that they will need a screwdriver to remove the four screws holding the vent cover in place. To TJ’s delight, **Tela** offers her nail file as a substitute tool. As TJ and Max leave to search for a hard hat to go with an inside-out gas mask as part of the disguise, Kep and Annie fall into another argument about animal rights before Querishi interrupts them and tells the group it is time to board the transport.

Kep realizes he must stall in order to give TJ time to hide in the vent. He tells Querishi that he wants to make a will before leaving. Annie supports the request, and in frustration Querishi finds paper and pens so they can quickly write their wishes. When they share what they wrote, Max’s note stands out as the most serious—he writes goodbye letters to his parents and to Grandpa Westgard. Kep realizes that Max truly believes the mission may be real and dangerous. Kep’s own will is more casual, leaving various personal items to teammates from his sports competitions.

Querishi quickly ushers the three onto the transport and explains that they will be supplied with money, maps, a compass, and detailed instructions for the mission. As the process begins, Kep hopes the delay they created was enough time for TJ to remove the vent cover and hide inside the machine.

### Character Perspectives

**Kep**  
**What We Learn.** Kep becomes the organizer of the group’s effort to smuggle TJ onto the machine. His quick thinking identifies the vent as a hiding place and later leads him to invent the idea of writing wills in order to stall for time.  
**Understanding of Events.** Kep still treats the mission with some skepticism, but he recognizes the stakes may be real enough to justify helping TJ carry out the risky plan.

**TJ**  
**What We Learn.** TJ remains determined to join the mission despite being excluded. He eagerly takes advantage of Tela’s nail file and prepares a disguise so he can hide inside the transport module.  
**Understanding of Events.** TJ continues to approach the situation with excitement and defiance, focusing more on getting onto the mission than on the danger involved.

**Max**  
**What We Learn.** Max’s seriousness becomes more apparent when he writes genuine goodbye letters to his parents and to Grandpa Westgard. His reaction shows he may believe the mission is truly dangerous.  
**Understanding of Events.** Max increasingly accepts that the time-travel mission could be real and behaves accordingly.

**Tela**  
**What We Learn.** Tela plays an active role in helping TJ’s plan by providing the nail file needed to remove the vent screws.  
**Understanding of Events.** By contributing the tool and cooperating with the plan, Tela shows she is willing to support the risky effort to get TJ aboard the machine.

**Minor Characters**

* **Annie** — Supports Kep’s idea of delaying the launch by writing wills, helping create time for TJ to hide.
* **Querishi** — Focused on moving the mission forward and grows impatient with the children’s delays, but reluctantly provides paper and pens for their wills.

### Narrative Devices in Youth Adventure Fiction

This chapter uses a common device in youth adventure fiction: the protagonists must deceive or outmaneuver the adults in order to participate in the story’s central action. Kep’s discovery of the vent, the need for a tool, the disguise for TJ, and the “writing a will” delay are all steps in a small conspiracy that allows the teenagers to take control of events.

Such sequences appear frequently in youth narratives because they reposition the adults from directors of the story to obstacles that must be circumvented. By successfully scheming around the adults, the young characters earn the right to become the true agents of the adventure.

By this point in the story, the narrative also fully adopts another common convention of youth adventure fiction: adults allow teenagers to participate in a mission that would realistically require trained adults and parental consent. While implausible in real-world terms, this device is necessary for the genre because it places the young protagonists at the center of the action. The adults still control the technology and the broader mission, but the success of the story depends on the teenagers themselves carrying out the critical tasks.
