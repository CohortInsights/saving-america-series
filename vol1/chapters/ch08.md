# Chapter 8

## Plot Summary

After the violent launch, the children awaken inside the dark transport vehicle as if they had been unconscious during the journey. Ignoring Annie’s instructions to wait for an “all clear” signal, they decide to leave the vehicle and investigate their surroundings.

Kep still assumes the situation is part of an elaborate competition. Tela discovers a silver case containing written instructions for their mission. The message declares that their actions may determine the future of their country, language that most of the group dismisses as melodrama—except for Max, who begins to take the warning seriously.

TJ theatrically quotes Benjamin Franklin’s warning: “We must all hang together, or we must assuredly hang separately.” The instructions explain that the group must reenact the warning rides associated with Paul Revere and William Dawes. They must conceal their identities and complete the mission within twenty-four hours, after which they can activate the time machine using a ten-digit code. The instructions also warn that dangerous people may be waiting for them and that their bodies may begin to break down if they remain in the past too long.

Tela cannot initially find the map and money that the instructions promise. In the darkness of the tunnel surrounding the vehicle, she eventually discovers a small key hidden in the case. Using the key, the group opens a door and climbs a ladder leading out of the underground structure.

Before leaving, Kep ties the key around his neck using twine he cuts with the knife Mule gave him. TJ continues searching for hidden cameras, convinced the scenario is staged. When no camp staff or reenactors appear, the group begins to wonder whether something is wrong with the supposed competition.

Kep finally tells the others everything he knows about the robberies, the stolen ring, Ramsey’s strange sleep-talking about a “time travel army,” and Detective Martinez’s visit to the camp. The group speculates about why they were chosen and whether their families might somehow be connected. TJ continues to mock the idea that they actually traveled through time, but Kep insists that Annie knows something about them that she has not revealed.

Their conversation is interrupted by the sound of a wagon approaching. An elderly driver allows the children to climb aboard after Max asks for a ride to Boston and Tela improvises a story about visiting a sick grandmother. During the ride the group forms a rough plan to divide the roles described in the instructions: TJ volunteers to carry out Revere’s ride, Tela offers to take the role of Dawes, and Kep and Max plan to handle the lantern signal at Old North Church.

As they approach Boston, carts become more common and Max notices something unsettling: the sky contains no airplanes or contrails, something that had always been visible at the reenactment camp.

When the wagon reaches a British checkpoint, the soldiers immediately question the driver about TJ, asking whether he is the man’s property. One soldier studies TJ while holding a paper and suddenly orders the other guard to seize the horses. TJ matches the description of a runaway slave, and the guard shouts for the sergeant:

“We’ve got that runaway.”

## Historical Themes and Details

**Paul Revere’s Ride.** On the night of April 18, 1775, Paul Revere first had to escape British-controlled Boston before beginning his warning ride. He secretly crossed the Charles River by boat under the guns of the British warship **HMS Somerset**, which was stationed nearby to prevent crossings. After reaching the opposite shore, Revere obtained a horse that had been prepared in advance and began his ride toward Lexington and Concord to warn colonial militias that British forces were marching to confiscate rebel weapons.

**William Dawes’s Ride.** Dawes simultaneously left Boston by land through the guarded Boston Neck checkpoint and rode toward Lexington. Although his warning ride was historically important, it never became as famous as Revere’s.

**Lantern Signals in Old North Church.** Patriots used lanterns in the church tower to signal the British route: “One if by land, two if by sea.” The lanterns served as a backup signal in case the riders were captured before delivering their warnings.

**British Control of Boston.** At the time, Boston was under British military control, and travel in and out of the city was monitored by soldiers stationed at checkpoints.

**Slavery in Colonial America.** The soldiers’ assumption that TJ might be a runaway slave reflects the racial realities of eighteenth-century society and immediately places him in danger.

* The mission instructions claim the children’s actions could determine the future of the United States.
* Is Kep right that Annie knows something that connects all of the children to the mission?
* The warning that their bodies may break down if they stay in the past too long suggests physical consequences of time travel.
* No camp staff or reenactors appear after the children exit the transport.
* The absence of airplanes or contrails in the sky suggests they may truly be in the eighteenth century.
* The soldiers’ reaction to TJ indicates the environment is not staged but historically authentic.
